"god save the animals" Fall 2022

Somehow, you knew that everything was a bit odd, and a bit unreal, and often fake, but because there was no other picture of the world, and you were so within this system you accepted it as normal and just went on.‚Äù - Adam Curtis, HyperNormalisation explained by Adam Curtis

Final project for my Photo II class. I have recently become interested in the concept of hyperreality; how technology is narrowing the gap between reality and simulation in a way that results in a very surreal and generally unstable perception of the world around us. At the same time, the structural inequalities in our society that maintain the social hierarchy of global capitalism have fractured the realities of many Americans by further widening the gap between the very wealthy and the poor, shrinking the middle class and decimating the american dream. In large cities like Boston, the income disparities driving this hierarchy are glaringly obvious, no more so than in Back Bay in the area surrounding Newbury Street, a street lined with commercial buildings ranging on one end fast-fashion stores like Urban Outfitters and TJ Maxx to the end of the road where luxury brands like Cartier and Chanel reside. The rabble who frequent the area vary widely from wealthy middle-aged women buying $15 smoothies and touting lululemon, college students at the vape stores, high schoolers and their coddling parents (the type to tie their yale alumnus sweaters around their shoulders) crowding into the Brandy Melville, an abundance of tourists taking selfies with the cherry blossoms in the spring and waiting in snaking lines to get into Zara (of all places). People fill the outdoor dining areas constructed hastily out of plywood feet away, laughing and drinking while less than ten feet away from them a homeless person is asking for money at an intersection or sleeping on the ground.

Two years ago, I was walking with my friend in new york city, the sun was setting and it was getting cold so the waiters on these outdoor patios were putting up heating lamps and I remember thinking how pretty the structure of the patio was; the wooden lattice frame covered in real vines and flowers, wrapped in warm string lights. As we walked past the eaters slurping down pasta and red wine at their tables, a man huddled inside a cardboard box. He had used newspapers to insulate himself and settled down to sleep for the night, through it was only around 7pm. Thinking back on it, the scene was a horrible lynchian manifestation, two realities coming together but refusing to blend, like oil and water. The question I always ask myself is whether the ignorance is a performance and I have yet to come up with a conclusive answer. However, through my photos, I wanted to illustrate a perspective of the chaotic, absurd reality of the world while combining even further surreal aspects in photoshop to disorient the viewer. These images depict the containers of social inequity that we are all trapped in and our ignorance in the face of it, adding in visual themes of corporate development and an apocalypse of biblical proportions that is invisible to everyone in the scene while the viewer remains helpless to intervene.
