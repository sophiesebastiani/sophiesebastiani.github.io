"The Myth of the Self-Regulatory Machine" Spring 2023




Final project for Advanced Video Production. I was drawn to this project, particularly the regulatory aspect, because of the magnitude of the issues surrounding 3d printing and artificial intelligence, the game of hot potato the supreme court is playing with congress over this problem, and the difficulties the 1st Amendment (+ others) poses to passing any legislation at all. I wish I could have spent an entire year creating this documentary and I still itch to work on it further but, as it stands now, the timing of this film is very important to me and the education that it proliferates. Acting as director, cameraman + crew, and editor this time was incredibly challenging but ultimately rewarding as it allowed complete control for me to tighten this vast observation into a cohesive narrative and pose some interesting questions about the freedom of internet usage in the future along the way.



"Where's Your Soul? The Failure of Urban Renewal" Fall 2022




Final project for Experimental Video Production. An analysis of how the current (as of 2023) housing crisis facing the US stems from the highly racialized failure of the 1950's and 60's movement of urban renewal by all levels of government. Similar to my video essay critiquing the NFT craze, this work pulls much inspiration from Adam Curtis' documentary work, especially in creating historical narrative through the collage of moving images.

The opening sequence of clips were filmed by me in Boston. The rest of the video incorporates appropriated footage, especially clips from contemporary news media, which I use to weave a visual timeline of the historical inequities in the housing market and address the cause of the same pattern of crashes in the market or great displacements like the one caused by the failure of this policy implementation.


"NFTs or The Exploitation of Speculative Markets" Spring 2022




Final Project + Paper (Read Here) for Decolonial Art History. Video includes ~9 minute roast of NFTs and the concept of speculative value in the art market through appropriated footage and a philosophical reflection on the experience of viewing art + a conversation with a lovely gentleman who works as a security guard at the Davis about going to the museum. The paper critiques the artificial price inflation of digital objects within a speculative market and the fabrication of false realities that conceal real-world problems like rising poverty and homelessness in America and abroad.



Stop Motion Visualizer to Japanese Breakfast's üé∂Posing in Bondageüé∂ Fall 2021




Created in Intro to 2D Design. I also have always loved the look of stop motion; it feels like an analog process (ie creating film stills) while being replicated by myself on a digital camera and it allows for creative manipulation between frames, which I tried to play with in messing with the time and sequence of movements. This assignment was all about movement paired with audio. I chose Japanese Breakfast's Posing in Bondage (2021) because all of the (what I like to call) *fluffiness* in the beat allowed for the rapid cuts necessitated by the many frames and its a song that I think really speaks to the basic need and desire for human companionship, a feeling I *tried* my best to replicate in less than 2 minutes üòÑ. I also used Adobe AfterEffects to create the animations.

Creating context, a visual narrative, through the spatial or sequential organization of static and moving images is so fun for me; I enjoy crafting a scene or series of clips that are densely-packed with information or feature small details that force the viewer to really pay attention, really look at the work. In this case, the visual metaphors are pretty obvious and simple but I think this early work really illustrates my interest in storytelling through collage, both spatially and temporally.
